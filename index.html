A comprehensive simulation dataset, 3,200 daylighting samples and 25,740 hourly energy configurations, was generated for a case study in Yazd, Iran, and used to train two high-accuracy ANN surrogate models capable of rapid performance prediction. Building Performance Simulation . Machine Learning
Parametric Simulation & ANN Surrogate Models for Real-Time Façade Prediction
+
Date: Jan 2024 – Jul 2025 Type: Thesis Supervisor: Dr. Morteza Rahbar

A simulation-driven machine learning framework was developed to enable real-time prediction of building energy and daylighting performance. Parametric simulations in Grasshopper generated two datasets: 3,200 daylighting samples and 25,740 hourly energy configurations. These datasets were used to train two ANN surrogate models capable of rapid performance prediction, enabling intelligent kinetic façade control without the computational burden of running full simulations at each decision step.

Step 1 Creating the Dataset
Case Study Definition
An open-plan office space in Yazd, Iran was selected as the case study due to its hot arid climate, which presents a challenging context for simultaneously managing solar gains, glare, and daylight availability. The shading system parameters were defined based on solar path analysis conducted in Climate Consultant.

Step 2 Training ANN Surrogate Models
Feature Engineering & Input Definition
Input features for each model were selected based on their physical relevance to the target output. The daylight model takes solar geometry, illuminance components, and louver angle as inputs to capture the combined effect of sun position and shading on daylight transmission. The energy model uses outdoor temperature, humidity, solar radiation components, and hour of day to represent the thermal and occupancy conditions driving energy demand. Model Development & Validation
A grid search was conducted to identify the optimal architecture and training parameters, evaluating combinations of hidden layers (1–4), neurons per layer (64–1024), learning rates (0.0001–0.01), batch sizes (32–256), and optimizers (Adam, RMSprop, SGD). Both models converged on a 3-hidden-layer architecture (512→256→64 neurons) with ReLU activations, trained on an 80/20 split with StandardScaler normalization and early stopping to prevent overfitting. The energy model achieved R²=0.98, confirming its reliability for real-time prediction. The daylight model achieved R²=0.98 for rUDI and R²=0.80 for Ev. The lower Ev accuracy could be attributed to dataset skew toward low-glare conditions and high solar variability during peak radiation hours.

Intelligent Control . Performance Evaluation
Multi-Objective Control Strategies for Kinetic Façade
+
Date: Jan 2024 – Jul 2025 Type: Thesis Supervisors: Dr. Amirreza Ardekani & Dr. Hoda Homayouni

Building on the ANN surrogate models developed in the previous phase, four intelligent louver control strategies were designed to jointly optimize glare prevention, daylight availability, and energy efficiency. Each strategy was evaluated against the conventional cut-off angle method across three annual performance dimensions, confirming that data-driven, multi-objective control significantly outperforms rule-based approaches.

Part 1 Intelligent Control Strategies
The control logic applied to the horizontal kinetic louvers is governed by occupancy status. During occupied hours, louver orientation is dynamically adjusted using the intelligent control strategies introduced below. During unoccupied periods, louver angles are set based on seasonal conditions. Four control strategies were developed for the occupied mode to select an optimal louver position at every hour.
Fig. 8 — Kinetic Shading Control Strategy Framework
Fuzzy Logic Control
Applies multi-valued reasoning through three fuzzy membership sets (Low, Medium, High) and eleven if–then rules, prioritizing glare prevention, then daylight availability, then energy efficiency.
Brute Force Search
Systematically evaluates all eleven louver positions at each time step. Configurations exceeding the Ev discomfort threshold are first filtered out; among the remaining options, the position yielding the highest rUDI is selected, with energy consumption used as a tiebreaker.
Weighted Scoring
Normalizes rUDI and energy consumption to a [0,1] range and applies a weighthed scoring function. An equal-weight baseline was tested alongside energy-dominant and daylight-dominant weighting scenarios. The equal-weight configuration delivered the best overall trade-off across glare reduction, sDA improvement, and energy savings.
Ev-Based Control
Selects the highest vertical illuminance value below the discomfort threshold, maximizing perceived daylight while preventing glare — an occupant-comfort-first strategy without explicit energy optimisation.
Part 2 Annual Performance Evaluation
Daylighting Performance
Annual UDI500–2000 and sDA300/50% were simulated using Ladybug Tools. Brute force search achieved the highest sDA value (0.98), while the weighted scoring strategy improved sDA by 10.6% over the cut-off angle baseline (0.66). All four AI-based strategies outperformed the conventional method on both daylighting metrics.

Conclusion Key Findings
All four AI-based control strategies outperformed the conventional cut-off angle method across daylighting availability, glare control, and energy efficiency. The weighted scoring system delivered the most balanced performance, achieving 55.9% glare reduction, 10.6% sDA improvement, and 7.3% total energy savings, while the brute force search maximized daylight availability with a 48.4% sDA improvement. Model accuracy was confirmed at R²=0.98 for both the energy model and the rUDI daylight output, with the Ev prediction achieving R²=0.80. The lower Ev accuracy could be attributed to dataset distribution skew toward low-glare conditions and high solar variability during peak radiation hours. Enriched high-glare datasets or LSTM architectures are identified as directions for future improvement. The integrated framework — combining parametric simulation data generation, ANN surrogate modelling, and multi-criteria control strategy — is transferable to other façade typologies, climates, and building programs.

Multi-Objective Optimization . Building Performance Simulation
Multi-Objective Optimization of Energy Consumption and Thermal Comfort using Genetic Algorithm
+
Date: Jun 2023 Type: Individual Academic Project Supervisor: Dr. Zahra Zomorodian

This project investigates the trade-off between energy efficiency and occupant thermal comfort in an open-plan office building in Shiraz, Iran. A simulation-based optimization workflow was developed in DesignBuilder, integrating sensitivity analysis, genetic algorithm optimization, and uncertainty analysis to identify Pareto-optimal design configurations that simultaneously minimize total energy consumption and thermal discomfort hours.

Step 1 Defining Optimization Objectives & Design Variables
Conflicting Objectives
High-performance building design requires balancing two competing goals: minimizing total energy consumption and maximizing occupant thermal comfort. Strategies that reduce energy use, such as lower setpoints, frequently compromise indoor comfort conditions, while comfort-driven decisions tend to increase energy demand. This conflict makes a single optimal solution impossible; the objective is instead to identify a Pareto front of configurations that represent optimal trade-offs across both dimensions.
Initiai Design Variables
Eight design variables were defined to cover the primary parameters influencing thermal performance and energy demand: (1) wall construction type, (2) roof construction type, (3) shading depth, (4) building orientation, (5) window-to-wall ratio (WWR), (6) cooling setpoint, (7) heating setpoint, and (8) natural ventilation setpoint. Both discrete and continuous parameter types were included to reflect the range of design decisions available at the early design stage.
Step 2 Sensitivity Analysis
A sensitivity analysis was conducted across 200 simulations to evaluate each variable's individual influence on total site energy consumption and thermal discomfort hours (ASHRAE 55 comfort standard). No single variable was found to dominate either objective; instead, multiple variables contributed meaningfully across both outputs. The four variables with the strongest combined influence were selected for the subsequent optimization stage: cooling setpoint, WWR, heating setpoint, and roof construction type.

Step 4 Uncertainty Analysis
An uncertainty analysis was conducted to evaluate the variability in simulation outputs attributable to operational and modeling uncertainties. Thermal discomfort hours were concentrated in lower-value ranges across the sample, while energy outputs exhibited moderate variability. Overall trends remained consistent with optimization outcomes, confirming the robustness of the Pareto-optimal solutions.

Conclusion Key Findings & Limitations
All Pareto-optimal solutions favored low WWR (20%), reflecting the influence of reduced glazing on thermal load management. Cooling setpoint demonstrated the strongest combined effect on both energy consumption and thermal comfort. High-performance roof assemblies (U = 0.267 W/m²K) appeared consistently among optimal configurations, underscoring the contribution of envelope thermal resistance to overall building performance. A key limitation of this study is the exclusion of visual comfort and daylighting performance from the optimization objectives, which may favour envelope configurations that compromise daylight availability. Future work should incorporate daylighting, visual comfort, and life-cycle cost as additional optimization objectives.

Daylight Optimization . NSGA-II Genetic Algorithm
Responsive Kinetic Façade with Diamond Modules
+
Date: Jul 2024 Type: Workshop Platform: DigitalFUTURES

This project investigates the performance of a responsive kinetic façade for an office building in Tabriz, Iran. The façade consists of 144 diamond-shaped modules that dynamically adjust their angles throughout the day to improve daylight availability, thermal comfort, and view quality. A multi-objective optimization workflow using the NSGA-II genetic algorithm determines the optimal façade configuration at two-hour intervals during winter days, and the resulting performance is evaluated against a static shading baseline.

Introduction Case Study
Space Characteristics
The case study is a south-facing open-plan office in Tabriz, Iran, a city characterized by cold winters and seasonal variation in solar angle, conditions that make dynamic façade adaptation particularly consequential.

Methodology Optimization
NSGA-II Multi-Objective Optimization
The optimization was implemented using Wallacei X with the NSGA-II algorithm. At each two-hour interval during occupied winter hours, the algorithm searched the full combinatorial space of module angles to identify configurations that simultaneously maximize daylight availability (DA₃₀₀), useful daylight illuminance (UDI), sky exposure (view quality), and Thermal Comfort Percentage (TCP). The multi-objective nature of the problem necessitated a Pareto-based approach, producing a set of non-dominated solutions from which the optimal configuration for each time step was selected.

Office Building Daylighting Retrofit
+
Date: Mar 2023 Type: Individual Academic Project Supervisor: Dr. Zahra Zomorodian

This project assesses and improves the daylight performance of an existing 12-story office building in Tehran, Iran. The existing daylighting and glare conditions were first assessed through simulation, revealing excessive daylight as the primary source of visual discomfort. Three alternative façade strategies were subsequently proposed and evaluated against daylight availability and glare risk.

Introduction Case Study & Simulation Details
Case Study Characteristics
The building under study is a tall corporate structure with predominantly open-plan office spaces in Tehran, featuring extensive glazing on its south façade. Occupied hours run from 8:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., and building occupants had raised concerns regarding poor visual comfort conditions in the office spaces.

Simulation Methodology
The 7th floor was selected as a representative sample for daylight and glare simulation. Simulations were conducted in Climate Studio using two performance metrics: Useful Daylight Illuminance (UDI) for daylight availability and Daylight Glare Probability (DGP) for glare risk.
Step 1 Base Case Analysis
Daylight
In the base case scenario, the average UDI across the 7th floor was 34%, with the majority of occupied hours falling in the excessive illuminance range. This confirmed that uncontrolled solar gain through the south-facing glazing is the dominant driver of poor visual conditions.
Fig. 26 — Base Case Average UDI Ddistribution
Glare
Glare conditions in the base case were similarly critical. Intolerable glare was recorded during 40% of occupied hours, with an additional 13% classified as disturbing and 10% as perceptible. Only 37% of occupied hours met the imperceptible threshold. These results confirmed that façade interventions targeting both daylight reduction and glare control were necessary.
Fig. 27 — Base Case DGP Distribution
Step 2 Proposing Façade Strategies
Strategy 1 — Overhangs
Horizontal shading elements (depth: 140 cm) along the south façade to intercept high-angle direct solar radiation and reduce excessive illuminance at workplane level.
Fig. 28 — Overhangs Configuration
Strategy 2 — Double Skin Façade
A second façade layer composed of 15 × 15 cm rectangular modules, optimizing natural light transmission while attenuating direct solar penetration and glare.
Fig. 29 — Double Skin Façade Configuration
Strategy 3 — Horizontal Louvres
Fixed horizontal shading devices (width: 35 cm) to control the incoming direct sunlight, improving visual comfort while maintaining a degree of connection to the exterior.
Fig. 30 — Horizontal Louvres Configuration
Step 3 Performance Evaluation of Proposed Strategies
Strategy 1 — Overhangs
This strategy increased average UDI to 56%, a 64% improvement over the base case. Intolerable glare hours were reduced by half relative to the base case, and the proportion of imperceptible glare hours increased from 37% to 57%.
Fig. 31 — Average UDI Distribution Using Overhangs
Fig. 32 — DGP Distribution Using Overhangs
Strategy 2 — Double Skin Façade
The double skin façade achieved the strongest daylight improvement across all three strategies, raising average UDI to 71%, a 108% improvement over the base case. Intolerable glare hours were reduced to 12%, approximately one quarter of the base case value, and imperceptible glare hours doubled from 37% to 74%.

Strategy 3 — Horizontal Louvres
Horizontal louvres improved average UDI to 64%, an 88% improvement over the base case. Intolerable glare hours were reduced to 18%, and imperceptible glare hours increased by 70%, from 37% to 63%.

Conclusion Best Strategy: Double Skin Façade
The double skin façade delivered the most significant improvement in both daylight availability and glare control. Average UDI increased from 34% to 71%, representing a 108% improvement, accompanied by a substantial reduction in excessive illuminance across the floor plate.
